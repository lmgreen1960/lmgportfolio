BARTENDR

Giving freelance bartenders the information they need to choose the gigs they want.

  • Date: August 2019

The problem

Freelance bartenders often have trouble finding open shifts. By building an app that provides bartenders with relevant information about the venue such as location, environment and specialty menu, we can help bartenders pick up additional shifts that fit their needs.

The process

01 Research

Designing for a specialized user group, like freelance bartenders, had an added challenge. At first, we had trouble gathering data. We adapted by expanded our user research to include a group with similar characteristics...baristas. Through inteviews and surveys with baristas as well as bartenders, we learned that beverage servers cared more about the overall experience and less about the money. They wanted details about the venue so they could choose gigs that fit their criteria. Our competitor analysis revealed that most shift matching tools were geared toward the event planner or the venue and not toward the worker. Research findings were documented as a proto persona, storyboard, journey map and user flow to lead us toward ideation.

02 Design

The MoSCoW method and "I Like, I Wish, What If?" were useful in prioritizing the features. These three key features filtered to the top as the most important.

  • location
  • venue/event details
  • an easy way to book a shift
The next step was to split up the features and sketch screens to create a prototype for user testing.




03 Test

During A/B testing we found that:

  • users preferred to look at a map for the location of a venue rather than a list because they preferred the visual orientation.
  • Users preferred a full calendar rather than a rolling bar with dates because they were better able to compare to their existing schedule.
  • Users preferred a simple and clean bar profile focused on the CTA for selecting open shifts with other information added to a dropdown list.

04 Refine

Further user tests with our high fidelity prototype yielded these findings:

  • Users needed prominent pins on the map to know what to do next.
  • They needed CTA or guidance when navigating the bar profile, especially on the speciality drinks section.
  • The drop down on the bar profile needed to be very prominent.


The conclusion

Our goal was to provide bartenders with a quick and easy way to find and select extra shifts with the option to learn more about the venue so they could be prepared to provide great service. The BARTENDR app does exactly that! We learned that when you have a specialized user group like freelance bartenders, you might need to survey a group that has similar characteristics. We used data and interviews with baristas as well as bartenders. We were able to translate the barista data and make it useful for our bartender application. My teammate conducted all of the user interviews because he had access to a good number of bartenders and baristas. This worked well for time management, but I really missed not being more involved in the user research. I discovered that it is one of my favorite parts of UX design. We had enough time to build out only a few features. We had to decide which of the many exciting features would be the most necessary to meeting our goal. Using a prioritization matrix was key to filtering and focusing our efforts.
